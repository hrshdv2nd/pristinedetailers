## Article Body

If you drive the Eastern Freeway, Eastlink, or Monash Freeway regularly, your car is absorbing stone chip impacts you mostly can't see yet. Road debris hits at closing speeds above 200km/h. A 5mm piece of gravel at that velocity carries enough force to punch through clear coat and into base coat in milliseconds. Paint protection film is the only product that stops this before it reaches your paint.

## Why Melbourne Freeways Are Particularly Hard on Paint

Not all freeways create equal chip risk. Melbourne's combination of ageing bitumen, heavy freight corridors, and constant roadworks gives it some of the worst chip conditions in Australia.

The Eastern Freeway between Chandler Highway and the Ringwood interchange is consistently high-risk for loose aggregate — particularly at on-ramp merge points where lane changes throw gravel sideways across traffic. Eastlink carries heavy freight south through Dandenong and sheds road debris from truck trays year-round. The Monash Freeway, especially between Toorak Road and Pakenham, combines aged bitumen sections with some of the highest truck volumes in Victoria.

Bayside corridors add a secondary problem: salt air from Port Phillip gets into fresh chip sites faster than inland areas, turning a surface chip into a rust point within weeks if left untreated.

## What PPF Actually Does to a Stone Hit

Paint protection film is a thermoplastic urethane layer — typically 150 to 200 microns thick — bonded to your car's exterior surfaces. That's roughly 10 to 15 times thicker than your clear coat.

When a stone hits unprotected paint, the rigid clear coat absorbs the energy by cracking, chipping, or cratering. When that same stone hits PPF, the film flexes and distributes impact energy across a wider surface area before returning to its original form. Premium PPF products are self-healing: minor surface abrasions close with ambient heat from the sun or a heat gun.

## Where to Apply PPF on a Melbourne Commuter Car

You don't need a full wrap to get meaningful protection. For daily freeway drivers, the high-impact zones are:

**Front bumper and bonnet leading edge** — These surfaces take around 80% of direct highway chip impacts. A partial front-end wrap eliminates most chip risk from following traffic.

**Mirror housings** — Exposed and directly in the airstream. Commonly chipped within the first 12 months on unprotected cars.

**Rocker panels** — Low-level road grit and spray from following vehicles. Important for anyone driving coastal routes.

**Headlight lenses** — Stone chips cause hazing and cracking. Replacement lenses on European vehicles often run $1,500 to $3,000. PPF extends their life significantly.

For Eastern Freeway or Eastlink commuters, the practical minimum is a full bonnet, front bumper, and mirrors. Full front-end coverage is the step up, and a full-body wrap is the right answer for prestige vehicles.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does PPF stop all chips?**
No product stops every impact at every angle. PPF dramatically reduces chip damage — absorbing the vast majority of gravel hits that would otherwise reach your paint. The film itself may show marks from direct impacts, which is the point: that damage is on the film, not your paintwork.

**Can PPF be added after a chip has already appeared?**
Yes, but we recommend a paint correction stage first. Applying PPF over existing chips seals in damage rather than correcting it.

**How long does PPF last on high-impact zones?**
Quality PPF on bonnets and front bumpers typically lasts 5 to 7 years on daily drivers. High-impact zones wear faster than body panels. We check film condition at service visits and advise when a section needs replacing.

## The Bottom Line

If you drive Melbourne freeways daily and your car is worth protecting, PPF on the front end is not optional — it's maintenance. The Eastern Freeway does not care what badge is on your bonnet. A 200-micron film does.
