Here's what most people don't realise: you don't need summer heat for a bird dropping to etch clear coat. At 12 degrees, the uric acid is still active enough to start damaging unprotected paint within 48–72 hours. If you have a ceramic coating, it wipes off with a damp cloth. If you don't, it's a paint correction job.

**Moisture is building up inside.**

Cold mornings, warm interiors, and a car that hasn't been properly dried out since summer is a recipe for mould. We start seeing it from late June onwards — almost always in the boot liner, under the floor mats, or in the rear seats. By the time it's visible, it's already spread.

## Copy Notes

**Why this works:**

- **Specific, not generic** — references actual Melbourne suburbs, actual species behaviour, actual chemical mechanism (uric acid). This isn't "winter can be tough on cars." It's specific enough that readers think "that's my street."
- **Bird dropping angle is urgent** — creates a real reason to act *this week*, not "when the time's right."
- **Mould is loss aversion** — nobody wants to discover mould in July. Framing the interior detail as prevention (not upsell) is more persuasive than any discount.
- **Ceramic pitch is low-pressure** — "winter is fine" removes the most common objection without forcing urgency.
- **Reply CTA** — the softest possible ask. Customers who aren't ready to book but reply become a warm conversation. These convert better than cold clicks.
- **Signed by a name** — "Tom" is more credible than "The Pristine Detailers Team." It's an email from a person.

- Members: Lead with "your membership covers this" — remove friction entirely
