## Article Body

Most people treat bird droppings like a minor inconvenience. Something to clean off when you get home, when you have a cloth nearby, when you get around to it.

That thinking is costing Melbourne car owners paint corrections they shouldn't need.

![Car paint surface showing environmental contamination damage](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1605559424843-9e4c228bf1c2?w=1200&q=80)

## What's Actually in a Bird Dropping

Bird droppings aren't just waste. They contain uric acid — a compound with a pH between 3.5 and 4.5, depending on the bird's diet. That puts them in the same corrosive range as vinegar. But unlike vinegar, bird droppings don't evaporate cleanly. They dry into a concentrated, sticky paste that keeps working on your clear coat long after the liquid has gone.

The damage mechanism is twofold. First, the acid starts breaking down the clear coat's chemical bonds. Second, as the dropping dries, it contracts and pulls at the surface beneath it — like a suction cup at a microscopic level. The longer it sits, the deeper both processes go.

On a hot Melbourne day — a typical summer afternoon in Toorak or Brighton where your car is sitting in direct sun — surface temperatures on dark paint can reach 60–70°C. At those temperatures, the acid reaction accelerates significantly. What might take an hour of damage in mild conditions can happen in ten minutes on a hot bonnet.

## Why Melbourne Is Particularly Bad for This

If you park under trees in suburbs like Hawthorn, Camberwell, or anywhere along the Mornington Peninsula coast, you already know the problem. Indian mynas, corellas, and fruit bats are prolific in Melbourne's leafy inner suburbs, and they tend to favour the same trees that provide shade — meaning your car is often parked directly beneath them.

The other Melbourne factor is the sun angle. Our UV intensity is among the highest in the world for a major city. That UV isn't just heating the surface — it's already working on your clear coat independently of the bird dropping. The two processes compound each other.

## How to Remove Bird Droppings Without Causing More Damage

Speed matters, but technique matters just as much. Wiping a dry dropping across your paint with a dry cloth is one of the worst things you can do — it drags abrasive particles through your clear coat like sandpaper.

The correct process:

**1. Soften first.** Saturate the dropping with water or a dedicated quick detailer spray. Give it 30–60 seconds to rehydrate.

**2. Blot, don't wipe.** Use a clean microfibre cloth folded into quarters. Press down and lift, rather than dragging across the surface.

**3. Repeat if needed.** Multiple gentle blots beat one firm wipe every time.

**4. Inspect the area.** In direct sunlight, look for any dullness or slight etching at the spot. A faint ring or texture change means surface damage that will need polishing to fix.

If you're out without a microfibre — even a clean, damp piece of soft paper towel is better than nothing. The goal is to get the acid off the surface and stop the reaction.

## What Happens If You Leave It

At 10 minutes, you're likely looking at minor surface softening that may polish out. At 30 minutes to an hour in direct sun, you're often dealing with a visible etch — a dull spot or slight cratering in the clear coat that requires machine polishing to correct. Leave it for a day or more, and the damage can penetrate through the clear coat into the base coat. At that point, you're looking at a respray.

This isn't worst-case scenario writing. We see this damage regularly, particularly on dark-coloured vehicles where the surface absorbs more heat, and on cars that park under the same trees every day.

## One Practical Defence

The single best thing you can do against bird dropping damage — aside from acting fast — is having a quality ceramic coating on your paint. Not because it makes your car immune to acid, but because it changes the surface properties significantly.

A coated car has a much harder, more chemically resistant surface layer. Bird droppings sit on top of the coating rather than bonding directly to the clear coat, which means they're easier to remove and slower to cause damage. The casual blot-and-go cleanup actually works, rather than grinding contamination into an unprotected surface.

![Ceramic coating being professionally applied to car paint](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1619767886558-efdc259cde1a?w=1200&q=80)

It won't eliminate the need to act quickly — ceramic coating is not a substitute for maintenance. But it buys you time and makes the cleanup safer when you can't get to it immediately.

The underlying rule stays the same: when you see a dropping, treat it like a small fire. Fast, calm, correct technique. The alternative is a paint correction job you didn't need.
