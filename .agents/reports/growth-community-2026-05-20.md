**Draft Post — r/CarsAustralia**

> **Title:** Melbourne-specific paint threats most people don't account for — what's actually damaging your clear coat
>
> Spent the last few years detailing cars across Melbourne and noticed the same damage patterns showing up repeatedly. Figured it was worth putting together what actually causes the most paint damage in this city specifically.
>
> **Bird droppings in Toorak/South Yarra/Brighton**
> The fruit bats and cockatoos in the inner east produce highly acidic droppings. The uric acid etches clear coat in as little as 8–12 hours in summer heat. Most people wipe it off after a day and think it's fine — the etch mark is already set by then. The fix: any bird dropping, wipe it off within a couple of hours with a damp microfibre. Don't dry-wipe — you'll drag the grit across the paint.
>
> **Stone chips on the Monash and EastLink**
> If you commute on either of these at speed, you're taking chips. It's just physics. The front bumper, bonnet leading edge, and A-pillars take the worst of it. Once the primer is exposed it starts to oxidise. Stone chip touch-up is fine short-term, but if you're doing freeway kays daily the only real solution is PPF on those panels.
>
> **UV and sea air along the bay**
> Bayside and Mornington Peninsula owners deal with accelerated clear coat oxidation from the combination of intense UV and salt air. Cars stored outside in Mt Eliza or Frankston without protection fade noticeably faster than the same model in Carlton. A ceramic coating helps here specifically — the UV-resistant chemistry holds up much better than wax.
>
> **Car park scratches**
> Inner-city Melbourne car parks are brutal. Concrete pillars, trolley lines, door dings. These are the hardest to prevent because they're impact-based, not chemical. PPF on the doors and lower panels absorbs most of this.
>
> Happy to answer specific questions about any of these — paint stuff is what I spend most of my week on.

**Draft Post — Tesla Owners Melbourne Facebook Group**

> **Something I see a lot of new Tesla owners get wrong about their paint — especially on white and black**
>
> After working on a lot of Teslas over the past couple of years, there's one thing I keep seeing that causes unnecessary damage:
>
> **Automatic car washes.**
>
> Tesla's paint is softer than most German cars. The rotating brushes at drive-through washes — even the "soft cloth" ones — leave swirl marks in the clear coat. They're not always obvious in dull light, but you'll see them immediately in direct sun or under a light. By the time most owners notice, they've got a full panel of micro-scratches that need machine polishing to remove.
>
> What actually works for apartment/street-parked Tesla owners:
> - **Rinseless wash** (like Optimum No Rinse) — bucket of water, no hose required, very forgiving on paint
> - **Waterless wash spray** for light dust between washes — just make sure the panel is actually dusty, not dirty, or you'll drag grit
> - **Two-bucket method** if you have access to water — one for your wash mitt, one for rinsing it
>
> If you've already got swirl marks on a black or dark Tesla, a single-stage machine polish will remove most of them. It's not a big job if caught early.
>
> Also — the front bumper and bonnet on Teslas chip badly. If you're on the freeway regularly, that front bumper is going to look rough within 18 months without some protection. Just something to factor in when you're thinking about what to do paint-wise.
>
> Happy to answer any questions — been seeing a lot of paint issues on Teslas lately that were easily preventable.

**Draft Post — Porsche Club Victoria / BMW Car Club Facebook Group**

> **The PPF vs ceramic coating question comes up a lot — here's how I actually think about it for performance/prestige cars**
>
> Gets asked constantly so figured I'd put together a proper answer rather than keep typing it out.
>
> **They solve different problems.** PPF (paint protection film) is a physical barrier — it absorbs stone chips, car park scratches, and minor abrasions. It self-heals surface scratches in heat. Ceramic coating is a chemical bond with your clear coat — it repels water, makes cleaning easier, adds gloss depth, and blocks UV. One is armour, one is a shield.
>
> **For track-day or spirited road cars:** PPF on the front bumper, bonnet leading edge, and A-pillars is close to essential if you're doing any real kays. The stone chip rate on a 911 or M3 at speed is significant. Ceramic on top of PPF is the full setup — the film protects structurally, the ceramic makes it easier to clean and keeps gloss up.
>
> **For weekend/show cars with minimal road exposure:** Ceramic coating alone is probably sufficient. If it's mostly garaged and you're not doing freeway kays, the stone chip risk is low and PPF may be over-engineering it.
>
> **For dark colours (dark blue, black, dark green):** Paint correction first, then ceramic. Dark colours show swirl marks and water spots badly. If you put ceramic on top of damaged paint, you're just locking in the scratches at a higher gloss.
>
> One thing I'd push back on: the idea that either of these is "set and forget." Ceramic still needs maintenance washes — it just makes them much faster. PPF needs inspection every year or two for lifting edges. Neither replaces actual care.
>
> Happy to go deeper on any of this — Porsches and BMWs are most of what I see week to week.

## Partnership Recommendation: European Prestige Dealerships (South Yarra / Toorak corridor)

**The angle:**
Independent European prestige dealerships — particularly used/pre-owned specialists in the South Yarra, Richmond, and Port Melbourne precinct — are a natural referral source. These businesses sell cars worth $80K–$300K to buyers who immediately ask "how do I protect this?" The dealership doesn't want to answer that question themselves. We do.

**Target partners:**
- Independent Porsche/BMW/Mercedes pre-owned specialists (there are several on Toorak Road and South Yarra)
- Ferrari/Lamborghini/Audi prestige used dealers in Port Melbourne and Richmond
- High-volume European new car franchises (BMW Melbourne, Mercedes-Benz Melbourne)

**What we offer them:**
1. A co-branded "New Car Protection Package" — customers who buy through the dealer get a preferred rate on a Pristine ceramic coating or PPF install. Dealer looks like they've solved a problem for the customer; we get a warm introduction at peak buying intent.
2. A branded leave-behind card in every handover pack — "Your new car's paint needs protection within the first 30 days. Here's who we trust."
3. Referral fee per completed job (optional — some dealers prefer branded packs over cash).

**What they get:**
- A trusted referral they can offer customers without liability
- Commission on referrals (or a preferred service arrangement for their own fleet/demo cars)
- Their customers are protected — which means fewer "the car had scratches when I bought it" disputes

**How to open the conversation:**
Don't pitch. Go in as a customer first — test drive something, meet the sales manager. Then follow up: "We do a lot of work on cars that come through your area. Would it make sense to chat about how we could handle new buyer protection packages together?" Bring a one-page summary and a before/after gallery on your phone.

**Secondary partnership: Independent European mechanics**
Shops like Absolute German (Moorabbin), Automech (Richmond), or any respected independent Porsche/BMW independent specialist. Their customers care about their cars. A referral card at the service counter with the mechanic's endorsement carries enormous trust weight — these mechanics have the relationship we're trying to build.
