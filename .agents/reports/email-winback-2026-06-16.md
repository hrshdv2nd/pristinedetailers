## Campaign Brief

**Segment:** Customers who booked once (ceramic coating, PPF, paint correction, or full detail) but have not returned in 90+ days.  
**Goal:** One re-booking. Not a membership pitch — just get them back in before spring.  
**Psychology:** Loss aversion + prospect theory. They've already paid for protection. That protection is being spent down right now, in winter, without maintenance. And spring UV is coming — the season that exposes every weakness in an ageing coating.  
**Offer:** Free interior detail (valued at $150) with any exterior service booked before 31 July 2026.  
**Sequence:** 2 emails. Day 90 → Day 97.

---

## Seasonal Context (June — Melbourne Peak Winter)

- Melbourne's June/July is the hardest stretch for car paint maintenance. Owners wash less often (cold, dark, wet), which means contamination accumulates unchecked.
- **Road spray near the bay:** Bayside, Brighton, St Kilda, Mornington Peninsula customers are getting salt-rich road spray on their lower panels and undercarriage daily. Salt doesn't wash off with rain — it bonds.
- **Bird droppings continue to be a threat.** Cold weather slows breakdown chemistry slightly, but the acid still etches through a neglected coating surface within 48–72 hours.
- **Condensation.** Cars sitting in driveways overnight collect moisture under door seals and in interior fabrics. By mid-winter, mould spores are active in around 20% of cars we see. It starts under floor mats and inside air vents — invisible until it smells.
- **Reduced UV doesn't mean safety.** The coating doesn't degrade from UV in winter — but chemical contamination (fallout, salt, tannins) builds unimpeded. When spring UV returns in September, it accelerates breakdown of an already-weakened coating surface.
- The gap between winter and spring is the highest-leverage maintenance window: clean and topped-up going in, the coating handles the September UV spike. Neglected going in, and the customer is looking at a paint correction by summer.

---

## Psychological Framework

**Primary lever: Loss Aversion**  
They spent real money — typically $800–$3,500 — on their coating or PPF. The frame is not "book again" but "your existing investment is at risk." People are more motivated to protect a past gain than to pursue a future benefit.

**Secondary lever: Availability Heuristic**  
Most customers don't picture what contamination does to their paint. Email 1 makes the invisible visible: condensation in the interior, salt bonding to lower panels, bird droppings working on a surface they can't see from the driver's seat.

**Tertiary lever: Temporal Discounting / Present Bias override**  
The loss frame ("your coating is degrading now") counters the natural tendency to defer maintenance until a later date. The time-limited offer (July 31) adds a prompt without manufactured urgency.

**Send timing:** Day 90 after last service  
**Goal:** Make the reader feel the cost of doing nothing. Not alarming — just honest.

---

**Subject line:** Winter is the quiet one. That's what makes it dangerous for your paint.

**Preview text:** You can't see it happening — but it is. A check-in on your coating three months in.

---

**Body:**

Hi [First Name],

It's been roughly three months since we came out — which puts you deep into Melbourne's winter with your [ceramic coating / PPF / paint correction] working on its own.

I want to give you an honest picture of what that looks like right now, because winter is the one most car owners underestimate.

Summer gets respect. UV damage is visible, people feel the heat on the paint, they book services. Winter feels benign because nothing dramatic happens. But the things winter does quietly are often the ones that shorten a coating's life the most.

**What's probably building up on your car right now:**

If you're anywhere near the bay — Brighton, Bayside, Mornington Peninsula, St Kilda — you're getting salt-rich road spray on your lower panels every time it rains. Salt doesn't rinse off. It bonds. By the time it's visible it's already done damage.

Bird droppings don't stop in winter. They're less noticeable because you spend less time standing outside looking at your car, but the acid is still working. On a maintained coating, they wipe off. On a coating that hasn't had a decontamination wash in three months, they start to bond permanently.

**The one people miss: inside the car.**

We've been seeing it on cars all month. Condensation gets into door seals, under floor mats, behind dash vents. It sits. By mid-winter, mould spores are active in a significant percentage of the interiors we work on — not visible, not smelling yet, but there. By spring, it's a problem.

None of this is an emergency. But the window where it's easy to fix is right now — before spring UV hits in September and accelerates breakdown on a coating that's been carrying winter contamination for months.

The coating you invested in is designed to last years. Maintenance is what makes that true.

If you want to know what your car probably needs at this point, just reply — happy to give you a straight answer based on what you had done and where you are.

Or if you're ready to book:  
[Book a service →](https://pristinedetailers.com.au/booking)

— The Pristine Detailers team

**PPF variant (Email 1):** Adjust Email 1 paragraph to reference edge-seal contamination rather than hydrophobic degradation. Salt and grime accumulating around PPF edges is a specific risk — if edges aren't kept clean, they start to lift. The core seasonal content (condensation, interior, spring prep) stays the same.

**Paint correction variant:** Customers who had a paint correction (not a coating) are at higher risk — correction removed surface damage but if no ceramic was applied afterward, the fresh clear coat is unprotected. Email 1 should acknowledge this: "You had a paint correction with us, which removed the existing surface damage. Without a protective coating applied since then, that clear coat is unprotected going into spring."

## A/B Test Suggestions

- **Email 1 subject line variant A:** "Winter is the quiet one. That's what makes it dangerous for your paint." vs. **variant B:** "Three months since we came out — here's what's probably happening to your car."  
  Tests seasonal curiosity hook vs. direct personal check-in.

- **Email 2 offer framing:** "Free interior detail ($150 value)" vs. "We'll clean your interior while we're there — on us."  
  Tests dollar anchoring vs. conversational/casual framing.

- **Email 2 urgency driver:** "School holidays" availability angle vs. "July 31 deadline" only.  
  Tests specific scarcity (slots) vs. date-based scarcity.
