Most Melbourne car owners arrive at this question the same way: they've just spent $60,000 on a new car and they've been told at the dealership to "get paint protection." But which one?

Paint protection film and ceramic coating are two completely different products that solve two completely different problems. Choosing the wrong one — or picking between them when you actually need both — is where most people go wrong.

Here's a clear breakdown.

## What Paint Protection Film Actually Does

PPF is a thermoplastic urethane film, typically 6–8 mils thick, that bonds physically to your paint surface. It acts as a sacrificial layer — a rock strikes your bonnet on the Eastern Freeway at 100 km/h, and the film absorbs the damage, not your clear coat.

Self-healing PPF goes a step further. The elastomeric topcoat uses heat — sunlight or warm water — to reform minor scratches and light swirl marks. It won't heal a deep gouge, but the gravel strikes and contact scratches that accumulate over years on Melbourne roads? They disappear.

PPF is the only product that physically stops stone chips. Ceramic coating does not do this.

## What Ceramic Coating Actually Does

Ceramic coating is a liquid polymer — typically SiO₂ (silicon dioxide) — that chemically bonds with your paint's clear coat. It cures to a hard, hydrophobic layer sitting at roughly 2–3 microns thick on top of your paint.

What it does well: it repels water, dirt, bird droppings, UV radiation, and most chemical contaminants. Rain sheets off cleanly. Bird droppings — which are highly acidic and can etch clear coat in under an hour during Melbourne's warmer months — sit on the surface rather than bonding to it. Regular maintenance washes take half the time.

What it does not do: stop a stone chip. The layer is hard but thin. Impact energy from a piece of gravel travels straight through it to the clear coat beneath.

## When to Choose PPF

If you drive Melbourne's freeways daily — the Eastern, EastLink, Western Ring Road — your front end takes constant stone chip exposure. PPF on high-impact zones (bonnet, front bumper, mirrors, headlights, A-pillars) is the most effective way to protect resale value on paint that's still in good condition.

New car delivery is the ideal time to apply PPF. Clean factory paint, no existing chips, film sits perfectly. Waiting until damage has appeared means paint correction first — adding cost before you've even started.

**Choose PPF when:** physical impact protection is your primary concern, you have a new or near-new vehicle, or you drive high stone-chip-risk routes regularly.

## When to Choose Ceramic Coating

Ceramic makes the most sense when your paint is in good condition and your primary threats are contamination, UV exposure, and maintenance time. If you park outdoors in Melbourne's inner east or bayside suburbs — areas with heavy tree cover, birds, and salt air — ceramic coating pays for itself quickly in reduced wash frequency and eliminated etching risk.

Ceramic also works as the ideal maintenance layer over PPF. In fact, that combination is the most common setup we recommend.

**Choose ceramic coating when:** UV and contamination are your main threats, you want to reduce time spent maintaining your car, or you want a hydrophobic finish that stays cleaner between washes.

## Why Most Melbourne Drivers Actually Need Both

The answer isn't PPF or ceramic — it's PPF on high-impact zones, with ceramic coating applied over the top.

This is the standard setup for any vehicle worth protecting properly. PPF handles physical impact on the bonnet, front bumper, and mirrors. Ceramic goes over the PPF and across the rest of the vehicle — doors, roof, guards, glass. You get impact protection where the car takes the most damage, and hydrophobic contamination protection across the entire surface.

It's not upselling. It's what each product was designed to do, working together.

## The Practical Call

If budget is a constraint, prioritise PPF on the front end of a new car over ceramic coating on an older one. Stone chips are permanent without a respray. UV fade takes years and can be managed with regular maintenance washes in the meantime.

If your car already has chips and you're considering a respray, do paint correction first — then PPF and ceramic on the fresh paint, in that order.

Melbourne's roads and climate are hard on paint. The Eastern Freeway alone accounts for more front-end damage than most drivers realise until they try to sell the car. A proper protection package costs a fraction of what a full respray does. Get it right once.
