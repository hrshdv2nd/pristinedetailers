## Article Body

If you drive regularly on Melbourne's Eastern Freeway, Eastlink, or the Monash, stone chips aren't a matter of if — they're a matter of when. Paint Protection Film (PPF) applied to your bonnet, front bumper, and mirrors stops chips before they reach your clear coat, preventing bare metal, rust ingress, and the kind of damage that shows up at trade-in time.

## What's Actually Happening to Your Paint at Freeway Speed

At 100 km/h, a piece of gravel doesn't land on your car — it hits it. The kinetic energy from a 5-gram stone at freeway speed is roughly equivalent to dropping a full can of paint from head height onto bare metal. Your OEM clear coat is typically 35–50 microns thick. A clean hit from road debris removes it instantly and often cuts into the base coat below.

Melbourne's road network makes this worse than most Australian cities. High traffic density, ongoing freeway infrastructure works, and loose aggregate on major routes create a near-constant chip environment for daily commuters and weekend drivers alike.

If you commute on the Eastern or use Eastlink regularly, your front end is accumulating damage most drivers don't notice until they look closely — or try to sell the car.

## PPF vs Unprotected Paint: What the Difference Looks Like After Two Years

| Impact Type | Unprotected Paint | PPF-Protected Paint |
|---|---|---|
| Small stone chips | Bare metal exposed, rust risk within weeks | Film absorbs impact, paint untouched |
| Large stone chips | Base coat damage, touch-up or respray needed | Film may mark, but paint underneath is intact |
| Road grit scratches | Accumulates in clear coat, dulls finish over time | Film takes the scratch; self-healing grades close minor marks with heat |
| UV damage on exposed areas | Accelerates — bare metal oxidises fast | Not applicable; paint is never exposed |
| 2-year resale appearance | Visible pitting on bonnet, chips on bumper | Front end presents near-new |

The difference compounds. One unaddressed chip in a high-moisture environment — say, near Port Phillip Bay or in the Bayside suburbs — can spread under the clear coat in six to twelve months.

## Which Zones to Prioritise First

Not every panel takes equal punishment. For Melbourne freeway drivers, the priority coverage zones are:

- **Full bonnet** — catches the majority of forward-projecting debris from the road surface
- **Front bumper** — low-angle chips, the most visible panel at point of sale
- **Side mirrors** — rocks deflected off trucks and adjacent lane traffic
- **Headlight lenses** — UV and chip damage on lenses is expensive to replace
- **A-pillars** — underrated, particularly on open freeway sections at speed

Full-body PPF makes sense for prestige vehicles and new car buyers who want comprehensive coverage. But partial application to high-impact zones gives most Melbourne commuters 80–90% of the protection at a fraction of the full-body cost. Start where the physics are working hardest against you.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does PPF stop all stone chips?**

No film stops every chip under every condition. A large piece of aggregate at very close range can penetrate even quality PPF. What PPF does is eliminate the vast majority of everyday freeway chips — the ones that accumulate invisibly over months and show up at trade-in. The film absorbs and disperses impact energy before it reaches your clear coat. That's the physics of it.

**Can PPF be added after a chip already appears?**

Yes, but the chip needs to be treated first. If there's bare metal or primer exposed, a technician will address that area before film application — otherwise you're sealing moisture against the metal, which accelerates corrosion. PPF over sound, undamaged paint is always the ideal scenario. Over an existing chip, it stabilises and protects the surrounding area but doesn't undo what's already happened.

**How long does PPF last on high-impact zones?**

Quality film on a front bonnet and bumper — the panels taking the most daily punishment — typically lasts 7–10 years with correct maintenance. High-impact zones do wear faster than protected side panels or roofs, so periodic inspection makes sense. Self-healing PPF grades recover minor surface marring with heat exposure but don't extend service life beyond their rated window. Replacing a partial zone is straightforward and doesn't require full-body reapplication.
