## Article Body

Stone chips on Melbourne's freeways aren't bad luck — they're physics. Loose gravel and road debris thrown at 100km/h punch through clear coat in milliseconds. On the Eastern Freeway, Eastlink, and Monash, most cars accumulate significant paint damage within the first three years of ownership. Paint Protection Film (PPF) is the only product that physically blocks that impact before it reaches your paint.

## Why Melbourne's Roads Are Harder on Paint Than You Think

Melbourne's freeway network sits on a foundation of crusher-run basalt — a porous, angular aggregate that breaks down under heavy traffic and stays in the water table beneath the road surface. This material works its way back onto the road constantly, especially after rain and during resurfacing projects.

The result: a semi-permanent supply of small, sharp projectiles on roads that are also high-speed.

| Freeway | Risk Level | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Eastern Freeway (Hoddle St to Ringwood) | Very High | Heavy truck traffic, constant resurfacing, concrete expansion joins |
| Eastlink (Ringwood to Frankston) | Very High | Open road speeds, aggregate loosened by heat cycles |
| Monash Freeway (CityLink to Dandenong) | High | Multi-lane merges concentrate debris in travel lanes |
| CityLink tunnels | Medium | High-speed, contained environment, tyre throw |
| Peninsula Link | High | 110km/h limit, rural gravel runoff at on-ramps |

One weekend run to the Peninsula isn't going to strip your bonnet. But 50 commutes on the Monash will.

## What Happens When a Stone Hits Unprotected Paint

When gravel hits at freeway speed, it doesn't just scratch — it displaces material. The sequence:

1. Impact fractures the clear coat
2. The chip exposes raw paint or primer beneath
3. Melbourne's humidity accelerates rust propagation under the clear coat
4. Within 6–18 months, the chip site expands and begins to lift

Chips near the front edge of the bonnet and lower front bumper are the most common. On a BMW 3 Series or Mercedes C-Class, a full bonnet respray costs $1,500–$2,500 at a competent panel shop — before you factor in colour-matching an ageing clear coat.

The case for PPF on a new car is fundamentally an insurance argument: pay once to protect the asset, or pay repeatedly to repair it.

## Which Zones to Prioritise First

You don't need to wrap the whole car to address freeway chip risk. The highest-impact areas are:

- **Full bonnet** — catches the majority of direct-trajectory debris
- **Front bumper** — especially the lower lip where road scatter concentrates
- **Front guards** — the wheel arch area takes significant splash-back
- **Headlights** — stone impact on polycarbonate causes irreversible crazing over time
- **Door leading edges** — catches debris thrown laterally by adjacent vehicles

For Melbourne freeway commuters doing 200+ km per week, a partial front-end wrap (bonnet, bumper, front guards, headlights) addresses around 90% of chip risk at roughly half the cost of a full wrap. It's where we start most consultations.

## FAQ

**Does PPF stop all stone chips?**
Not every one. A large piece of aggregate at very close range can penetrate the film. But PPF stops the vast majority of freeway chips — the small, high-velocity debris that accounts for most paint damage — completely.

**Can PPF be added after a chip already appears?**
Yes, but the existing chip stays under the film. PPF prevents new damage; it doesn't repair old damage. If your paint already has chips, we'll assess whether a touch-up is worthwhile before installing film, so you're not sealing the damage permanently underneath.

**How long does PPF last on high-impact zones like the bonnet?**
Quality film carries a 10-year manufacturer warranty on high-impact zones. In practice, slight yellowing at the 7–8 year mark is the most common reason for replacement — the protection itself remains effective until then.
