## Ad Variants

### Variant 1 — No Drop-Off Differentiator (Angle: The thing that stops people booking a detailing studio)

**Primary text (125 chars):**
> No workshop. No drop-off. A certified mobile team comes to your home and applies a nano-ceramic bond that lasts up to 8 years.

**Headline (25 chars):**
> Ceramic Coat. No Drop-Off

**Description (26 chars):**
> From $750. We Come To You.

**Why this works:** The single biggest reason Melbourne car owners don't book ceramic coating at a fixed studio is the logistics — they need to drop the car off, arrange transport, lose the vehicle for a day. "No workshop. No drop-off." pre-empts that objection in the first five words before the audience has a chance to form it. The rest of the copy describes exactly what they get instead: a certified team at their home, a technical explanation ("nano-ceramic bond") that signals this isn't a DIY spray-on product, and a duration that anchors the value ("up to 8 years"). The copy is 125 characters exactly — full primary text visible without the "See more" truncation in feed. No claim about being "the best" or "Melbourne's premier" — two phrases Pristine's brand voice explicitly avoids.

**Note on character count:** Primary text is exactly 125 chars — confirm platform render before launch; some placements may apply truncation at a lower threshold on mobile. If truncation is confirmed, trim to "No workshop. No drop-off. Certified mobile team, your home, nano-ceramic bond, up to 8 years." (93 chars).

## Retargeting — /booking Page Abandoners (Ceramic Coating Focus)

For users who visited `/services/ceramic-coating` or `/booking` with ceramic coating selected but did not complete (window: 7 days):

**Primary text (103 chars):**
> Still thinking about the coating? 4.9 stars. 2,400+ cars. From $750 — 8-year protection. Book a quote.

**Headline (22 chars):**
> 8 Years Of Protection.

**Description (23 chars):**
> From $750. No Drop-Off.

**Targeting window:** 7 days post-visit
**Frequency cap:** 2× per week — ceramic coating is a high-consideration purchase; over-serving triggers doubt, not urgency. At $750+, the buyer is deliberate, not impulsive
**Exclusions:** Anyone who completed a booking after visiting; existing ceramic coating customers (last 12 months); current membership subscribers (route them to a separate upsell sequence)

**Why this works:** A $750 ceramic coating is not an impulse purchase. The person who got to the booking page and left is not against the product — they paused to think. The retargeting message respects that deliberation: it doesn't fabricate scarcity, doesn't discount, and doesn't push with countdown timers. It simply restates the strongest proof points (4.9 stars, 2,400+ cars, 8-year protection) and closes with a low-friction action ("book a quote" rather than "book now"). "No Drop-Off" in the description reminds the abandoner of the unique advantage that got them to the page in the first place.

## Creative Direction Notes

**Image/video guidance:**
- Hero visual: Certified technician applying ceramic coating to a dark Porsche or BMW in an Inner East residential driveway — morning light, garage setting, professional equipment visible. The "come to you" setup (van, hose, equipment on the drive) should be visible. This is not a studio shot. Melbourne homes with established landscaping and double-brick garages signal suburb recognition to the Toorak/Hawthorn ICP.
- Water-bead video (5–10 sec loop): Close-up of water rolling off a freshly coated bonnet in sheets. No music. No voiceover. Pure product performance. Run as an Instagram Reel or Story. The visual sells the technical claim without a single word. Native-feel, not polished — filmed on location, not in a studio.
- Before/after static (carousel): Two panels — same car, same angle. Left: paint with swirl marks, water spots, oxidation. Right: after ceramic coating, deep gloss, water beading. Caption: "Same car. 48 hours later." Caption on right panel: "Certified ceramic coating. We came to their garage in Toorak." Location specificity matters.
- Testimonial card (V3): Marcus T.'s quote over a Porsche 911 GT3 in a Toorak garage setting. Real location, real car type — no stock imagery. The 911 GT3 silhouette is recognisable to the ICP and functions as a visual trust signal independent of the copy.
- Avoid: generic overhead shots of a wet car on a clean driveway; studio product photography; smiling technician holding a cloth; any stock photography that could be from any detailing brand anywhere in Australia.

**Format priority for Ceramic Coating:**
1. Instagram Feed — V2 vehicle-specific variants; the visual-first format rewards the car + suburb specificity; the ICP in Toorak and South Yarra are Instagram-heavy
2. Facebook Feed — V1 (no drop-off) and V3 (testimonial); broader age demographic reach; works well for the 40–55 segment who may be less active on Instagram
3. Instagram Reels — 5–10 sec water-bead video loop; native format, high engagement; runs against V1 copy overlaid on-screen
4. Stories — retargeting only; single-image booking abandoner copy with swipe-up to booking page

**Copy test plan (weeks 1–2):**
- Run V1 (no drop-off/convenience) vs V3 (testimonial) as cold audience in both Inner East and Bayside — measure CTR and cost-per-click-to-ceramic-page
- V2 vehicle variants run per interest group as challenger sets — determine which vehicle/suburb combo produces the lowest CPA
- Retargeting budget is separate (estimated 10–15% of total ceramic budget); exclude from prospecting measurement pool
- Kill lowest-performing cold variant at 1,000 impressions per ad set; consolidate budget to winner; test a fresh creative on the same winning angle in week 3

## Context Notes

- **Melbourne-specific hooks used:** Toorak, South Yarra, Brighton, Richmond, Eastern Freeway, Monash Freeway, bay winds, UV all named; Marcus T.'s Porsche 911 GT3 with verbatim testimonial from product marketing context; vehicle + freeway + suburb combinations built from actual Melbourne geography
- **Tone check:** Confident and expert; no exclamation marks; no "best in Melbourne"; no "passionate about cars"; copy states what the product does technically ("nano-ceramic bond", "8-year warranty", "water rolls off in sheets") and lets Marcus T.'s actual words do the emotional selling
- **Ceramic vs PPF clarity:** No mention of PPF in any variant. Buyers searching for ceramic coating are at a different price and decision point. Cross-sell belongs on the landing page after the buyer has committed to the ceramic decision — not in the ad where it dilutes intent and confuses the click
- **Price transparency:** $750 is stated in all variants. Pristine's ICP is not price-sensitive at this level — a $120K car owner does not filter on $750. But naming the price upfront disqualifies price-shoppers before the click, saving budget and reducing bounce rate on the landing page
