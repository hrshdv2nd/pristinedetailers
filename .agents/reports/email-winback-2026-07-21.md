## Campaign Brief

**Segment:** Customers who booked once (ceramic coating, PPF, paint correction, or full detail) but have not returned in 90+ days. Exclude anyone who responded to (booked or opted out of) the May 2026 or June 2026 win-back sequences.  
**Goal:** One re-booking before spring arrives. Not a membership pitch — just get them back at the right moment.  
**Psychology:** Loss aversion + temporal scarcity. The framing shifts from the June campaign ("winter is still happening") to a forward-looking frame: "Winter is almost over — but what it's left on your paint will follow you into spring." The pre-spring window (late July / early August) is the highest-leverage maintenance moment: fix the winter accumulation now, and the coating performs through September's UV spike. Leave it, and three months of contamination gets baked in by the first warm weekend.  
**Offer:** Free paint decontamination (clay bar treatment, normally $80–$120 depending on car size) included with any booking made before **15 August 2026**.  
**Sequence:** 2 emails. Day 90 → Day 97.

## Seasonal Context (July — Melbourne Late Winter / Pre-Spring)

- Late July in Melbourne: average overnight lows around 6–7°C, daytime highs of 13–14°C. The coldest fortnight of the year. Cars have been sitting through roughly 10–12 weeks of winter by the time this email reaches them.
- **Bonded contamination.** Winter road spray doesn't wash off with rain or a quick rinse. Brake dust, industrial fallout, exhaust particulates, and salt from coastal roads embed into the paint surface or coating over weeks. By late July, most cars haven't had a proper decontamination wash since before winter — meaning 3+ months of this has bonded to the surface.
- **The pre-spring window.** Melbourne's UV intensity starts climbing in late August and peaks through September–November. For cars with a ceramic coating or PPF, spring UV is when the previous 3 months of maintenance (or neglect) becomes visible. A coating carrying bonded winter contamination into spring is a coating that's already partially compromised — the UV accelerates degradation of a surface that's no longer clean.
- **Clay bar decontamination** is the specific treatment that addresses this: it mechanically removes embedded contamination that pH-neutral washing can't touch. It's the correct intervention at this point in the year — not just a wash, not just a top-up, but a full reset of the surface before spring conditions arrive.
- **Bayside and coastal customers** (Brighton, St Kilda, Mordialloc, Mornington Peninsula): salt spray from Port Phillip Bay has been accumulating on lower panels since May. Salt that's been bonding for 10+ weeks requires clay bar treatment to fully remove. A wash won't do it.
- **The window is genuinely narrow.** Late July to mid-August is optimal. Do it too late (September onward) and UV has already been working on a contaminated surface for weeks. Do nothing, and the coating that cost $750–$3,500 starts its second year at a deficit.

## Psychological Framework

**Primary lever: Loss Aversion (temporal framing)**  
They've already spent real money on protection. The frame is not "book again" but "the protection you paid for is about to be tested, and it's not ready." People work harder to protect an existing investment than to gain an equivalent future benefit. The temporal urgency here is real — late July/early August is the correct time to do this, not because of an artificial deadline but because of what happens to paint in September.

**Secondary lever: Peak-End Rule / Contrast**  
They had a great experience at their first service. The email acknowledges that — "your coating has been doing its job" — and contrasts it with what's accumulating on the surface now. The gap between "how good it was" and "where it's headed without action" is the motivating contrast.

**Tertiary lever: Exclusivity / Right-Timing**  
This isn't a blanket offer — it's framed as something that makes sense specifically at this point in the year. The message isn't "come back anytime" — it's "come back now, because now is the right time." That framing respects the reader's intelligence and doesn't feel like a generic sales prompt.

## Email 1 of 2

**Send timing:** Day 90 after last service  
**Goal:** Make the reader picture what 3 months of Melbourne winter has left on their car. Not alarming — just specific and honest. The ask is soft: "reply if you want to know where you stand."

---

**Subject line:** Three months of Melbourne winter is sitting on your paint right now.

**Preview text:** Winter's almost done — but what it leaves behind is the bit most people don't deal with.

---

**Body:**

Hi [First Name],

It's been around three months since we came out, which means your [ceramic coating / PPF / paint correction] has been working through one of Melbourne's harder winters on its own.

I want to give you an honest picture of where things probably stand, because late July is actually a more important moment for your car than most people realise.

Here's why.

**What accumulates over a Melbourne winter that a normal wash won't remove:**

Brake dust, industrial fallout, and road spray don't rinse off with water — they bond into the paint surface or coating over time. By late July, after 10–12 weeks of winter driving, most cars have a layer of embedded contamination that you can't see clearly but can feel if you run a clean finger across a dry panel. That gritty, rough texture isn't dirt — it's contamination that's bonded into the surface.

For cars near the bay — Brighton, St Kilda, Mordialloc, Mornington Peninsula — there's also salt from coastal road spray in that mix. Salt that's been sitting and bonding since May.

**Why right now is the window:**

Melbourne's UV intensity starts climbing in late August and peaks through September. For coated or protected paint, spring UV is when the last three months of maintenance (or neglect) becomes visible. A coating carrying three months of bonded winter contamination into spring is already compromised. The UV doesn't cause the problem — it just reveals it and accelerates it.

The specific treatment for this is clay bar decontamination: it pulls bonded contamination out of the surface mechanically, in a way that washing can't. Think of it as a reset — the coating goes into spring working properly, rather than already partially buried under what winter left behind.

If you want a straight read on where your car probably stands right now, just reply. Tell me what you had done, roughly where you are, and how much driving you've been doing this winter — I'll give you an honest answer on what it likely needs.

Or if you're ready to book:  
[Book a service →](https://pristinedetailers.com.au/booking)

— The Pristine Detailers team

## A/B Test Suggestions

- **Email 1 subject line:**  
  - Variant A: "Three months of Melbourne winter is sitting on your paint right now." (specific, factual)  
  - Variant B: "Late July is the right time to sort your car. Here's why." (timing hook, curiosity-led)  
  Tests specificity/loss-framing vs. right-timing/exclusivity framing.

  - Variant A: Include the "run a finger across a dry panel" line  
  - Variant B: Remove it, keep technical  
  Tests sensory/tactile engagement vs. clean technical explanation.

  - Variant A: "We'll include free paint decontamination ($80–$120 value)" (dollar-anchored)

  Tests value anchoring vs. conversational expertise signal.

  - Variant A: August 15 date only
