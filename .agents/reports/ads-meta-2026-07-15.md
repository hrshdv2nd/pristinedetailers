## Ad Variants

### Variant 1 — Pain Point (Angle: Melbourne freeways are doing this to your clear coat right now)

**Primary text (107 chars):**
> The Eastern, the Monash, the M1. Melbourne freeways chip your clear coat. Ceramic seals it. Book this week.

**Headline (25 chars):**
> Ceramic Coating Melbourne

**Description (26 chars):**
> From $750. We come to you.

**Why this works:** Three named freeways before the first full stop — Melbourne car owners who commute or drive the Peninsula recognise exactly what's being described. "Chip your clear coat" is technically specific without requiring a glossary; it names the real mechanism of damage rather than vague paint wear. "Ceramic seals it" is a declarative answer — four words, no hedging, no "can help with." The CTA ("Book this week") is soft urgency without a fabricated countdown; it frames booking as a near-term action, not an indefinite consideration.

This variant runs as cold audience prospecting for buyers who haven't heard of Pristine. The freeway hook activates an anxiety that exists before they knew ceramic coating was the solution. They'll click to understand how it works, land on a page that explains the bonding chemistry, and convert or retarget.

**Vehicle rotation plan:**
- V1a: "The Eastern, the Monash, the M1." — generic; runs against broad car care interests and postcode targeting
- V1b: "The Eastern chips your GT3's clear coat every morning. Ceramic seals it. Book this week." (89 chars) — Porsche interest group + Hawthorn/Toorak postcodes
- V1c: "The Monash chips your M4's clear coat every night. Ceramic seals it. Book this week." (84 chars) — BMW interest group + South Yarra/Richmond postcodes
- V1d: "The Mornington Peninsula is shredding your Defender's clear coat. Ceramic seals it." (84 chars) — Land Rover interest + Mornington Peninsula postcodes

Same structure, same CTA. Swap the named car and the road to match the interest/postcode set exactly.

**Why this works:** "Your Porsche" opens with ownership — not a vague "car owners" or "people with nice cars." It names the vehicle, which immediately signals to a Porsche owner that this ad was written for them, not for everyone. The Eastern Freeway is Toorak's artery to the CBD and back — it's the correct road for this audience.

### Variant 3 — Social Proof (Angle: "Better than factory" — from a Porsche GT3 owner)

**Primary text (115 chars):**
> "The finish on my GT3 is better than factory. Water rolls off in sheets." — Marcus T., Porsche GT3. That's ceramic.

**Headline (23 chars):**
> 8-Year Ceramic Warranty

**Description (26 chars):**
> Mobile. Toorak to Bayside.

**Why this works:** Opens with a verbatim testimonial from the product marketing context — unedited, specific, from a named vehicle. "Better than factory" is a credibility claim that only someone who has actually experienced high-end factory paint can assess — it lands authentically because it sounds like something a Porsche owner would say, not something a marketer would write. "Water rolls off in sheets" is a sensory description of the hydrophobic property — visual, memorable, verifiable in person.

"That's ceramic" closes the testimonial without explanation. It doesn't say "this is what our ceramic coating service achieves" — it says "that's ceramic." The confidence is the claim.

The headline anchors on the warranty: "8-Year Ceramic Warranty" is the proof point that transforms a one-off service into a long-term decision. For a $50K–$200K car, an 8-year manufacturer-backed warranty changes the value calculus from luxury spend to asset protection.

**Retargeting application:** This variant is the strongest retargeting creative for people who visited the ceramic coating page but didn't convert. The Porsche GT3 reference is aspirational but not alienating — a buyer with a $70K Range Rover still identifies with Marcus T.'s mindset. Pair with the booking-abandonment sequence below.

## Retargeting — /booking Page Abandoners (Ceramic Coating)

For users who visited `/services/ceramic-coating` or initiated `/booking` with ceramic coating selected but did not complete (window: 7 days):

**Primary text (108 chars):**
> Still thinking about the ceramic? We set up in your driveway. No workshop. 8-year warranty. Book in 2 minutes.

**Headline (22 chars):**
> Your Quote Is Waiting

**Description (26 chars):**
> Mobile. We come to you.

**Targeting window:** 7 days post-visit  
**Frequency cap:** 3× per week — ceramic is a considered, mid-to-high ticket decision; over-serving triggers doubt or ad blindness, not urgency  
**Exclusions:** Suppress anyone who completed a booking post-visit (immediate exclusion on conversion event); also suppress existing ceramic customers (recoat cycle is 5–8 years)

**Why this works:** "Still thinking about the ceramic?" opens in their voice — they are, literally, still thinking about it. It doesn't push; it acknowledges. "We set up in your driveway" is the key objection addressed — the most common drop-off reason for ceramic is assuming it requires a workshop, a full-day drop-off, and arranging a lift home. "No workshop" removes it explicitly. "8-year warranty" closes the value loop — this isn't a spend, it's a 5-year protection decision. "Book in 2 minutes" removes the final friction: this is a low-commitment action even if the decision itself is considered.

No discount offered. The friction holding this buyer back is uncertainty about the mobile delivery quality — not price. Discounting would weaken the premium positioning and attract the wrong segment.

## Creative Direction Notes

**Image/video guidance:**
- Hero visual: Technician setting up at a residential driveway — Toorak or Brighton street context, period-appropriate home, one of the target vehicles (Porsche, BMW, Range Rover) in frame. The car should be centre-frame. The technician is working, not posing. Morning light (before a work day begins) — this is a service that fits around the customer's schedule.
- Hydrophobic demonstration video (10–15 sec, no audio): Water poured slowly over a horizontal bonnet panel, rolling off in wide sheets without beading into droplets. No text, no voiceover — the visual is the claim. Final 2 seconds: "Ceramic Coating Melbourne — Pristine Detailers" as title card. This format works as an Instagram Reel thumbnail-to-play, stops the scroll without sound.
- Before/after split: Left — paint in raking light showing swirl marks and water spots from Melbourne winter. Right — same panel post-ceramic, high-gloss, water sheeting off. No filter difference between panels — the result must read as real, not photoshopped.
- "Technician in Toorak" carousel: Frame 1 — technician arriving at a recognisable Inner East street. Frame 2 — decontamination wash. Frame 3 — ceramic application. Frame 4 — finished car. Frame 5 — "We come to you. Book now." Five frames, no studio, real residential context.
- Avoid: composite before/after images that look retouched; stock images of pouring ceramic liquid from a bottle (too generic and sells the product, not the service); outdoor studio setups without Melbourne context.

**Format priority for Ceramic Coating:**
1. Instagram Feed (static) — V2 identity variants; visual-first, vehicle-specific; stops the scroll for owners of the named marque
2. Facebook Feed (static + carousel) — V3 social proof (testimonial); V1 pain point; broader reach to the demographic who hasn't yet searched for ceramic coating
3. Instagram Reels (video) — Hydrophobic water sheet demo, 10–15 sec; no voiceover; pure visual claim
4. Stories — retargeting only; booking abandoner copy with direct booking CTA; swipe-up friction matches the low-friction message

**Copy test plan (weeks 1–2):**
- Cold: Run V1 (freeway pain point, generic) vs V3 (Marcus T. testimonial) as the head-to-head for cold prospecting — measure CTR and cost-per-click-to-ceramic-page; winner takes the majority of prospecting budget in week 3
- V2 vehicle rotation variants run per interest group as challenger sets — test whether named vehicle + named road (V2a: Porsche/Eastern) outperforms generic V1 on a per-interest-group basis
- If V2 variants beat V1 on CTR at 1,000 impressions per set, build vehicle-specific versions of V1 and V3 as well (the named car hook appears to have legs across all three angles)
- Retargeting budget is isolated; exclude retargeted audiences from prospecting measurement pool

## Seasonal & Strategic Context Notes

- **Melbourne-specific hooks used:** Eastern Freeway, Monash, M1, Mornington Peninsula Freeway, Punt Road named across variants; Toorak, South Yarra, Brighton, Hawthorn named in targeting and in V2 description; Marcus T. testimonial contextualised as a GT3 owner (aspirational for the Inner East ICP)
- **Tone check:** V2 ("stone chips aren't bad luck — they're physics") is brand-voice correct — confident, factual, no apology. V3 testimonial is verbatim, unembellished — no addition of marketing language around or after the quote. V1 is specific about the damage mechanism, not vague about "protecting your investment."
- **July seasonal angle:** Mid-winter in Melbourne is a prime decision window for ceramic coating. Post-winter paint damage (grit, road spray, car park oxidation from wet roads) is visible, especially in low winter sun. The buyer who acted on week 28's paint correction campaign and noticed the damage is now the most qualified prospect for ceramic. Paint correction → ceramic is the natural upsell sequence; retarget last week's correction campaign clickers with V3 or the retargeting message above.
- **Positioning vs. PPF:** Ceramic is the correct product recommendation for buyers primarily concerned with UV, minor scratching, contamination, and gloss. PPF is the correct recommendation for buyers primarily concerned with stone chips and physical impact damage. If a buyer asks about both, the honest answer is to layer PPF on high-impact panels (bonnet, A-pillars) and ceramic on top. The landing page should frame this clearly — don't leave buyers confused by positioning ceramic as a chip stopper when PPF is the correct product for that concern.
