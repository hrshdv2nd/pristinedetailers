## Article Body

Most people notice the dropping, make a mental note, and drive on. By the time they get around to cleaning it off — an hour later, or the next morning — the damage is already done.

This isn't scaremongering. It's chemistry.

![Close-up of car paint surface showing the importance of paint protection](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1604754742629-3e5728249d73?w=1200&q=80)

## What Makes Bird Droppings So Destructive

Bird droppings are acidic — typically sitting between pH 3.5 and 4.5 depending on the bird's diet. For context, battery acid sits at pH 1. Your car's clear coat is designed to handle environmental exposure, but not sustained acid contact.

Here's what happens at a molecular level: the uric acid in bird droppings begins etching the clear coat almost immediately on contact. As the dropping dries and shrinks in the sun, it contracts — pulling the softened clear coat with it. The result is a shallow crater in the paint surface, sometimes called "acid etching." It looks like a cloudy ring or a slightly raised texture even after the dropping is removed.

In Melbourne's summer heat — particularly in suburbs like Toorak, Brighton, and St Kilda where you're often parking under established trees near cafes or beach-side strips — the sun accelerates this process dramatically. A dropping that might take four hours to etch paint in cooler weather can cause visible damage within 10 minutes on a hot Melbourne day when your car's bonnet is sitting at 60°C+.

## The 10-Minute Rule (and Why It Matters Here)

Under direct Melbourne sun in summer, you're working with a narrow window. Here's a rough timeline of what's happening to your paint:

- **0–10 minutes:** The dropping is still wet or beginning to dry. Risk of etching is present but minimal.
- **10–30 minutes:** Partial drying. Acid concentration increases as moisture evaporates. Etching begins.
- **30+ minutes:** Significant drying. The acid is now concentrated and working actively on your clear coat.
- **Hours:** The dropping has contracted and physically lifted or distorted the clear coat surface.

The 10-minute window isn't absolute — it depends on temperature, direct sun exposure, the bird species, and the surface temperature of your paint. But it's the practical benchmark to work with.

## How to Remove Bird Droppings Without Making It Worse

Most people make one critical mistake: they wipe. Don't wipe.

Wiping a dry or partially-dry dropping drags the abrasive uric acid crystals across your clear coat, adding micro-scratches to the etching damage. Instead:

1. **Soak first.** Use a clean microfibre cloth dampened with water — distilled water is ideal, but tap water works. Lay it over the dropping for 30–60 seconds to rehydrate it.
2. **Lift, don't wipe.** Press the cloth down and lift straight up. Repeat until the material is gone.
3. **Use a dedicated bird dropping remover if you have one.** Products like Auto Finesse Spritz or Gtechniq W6 are formulated to break down the uric acid without harsh scrubbing. They're worth keeping in the glovebox.
4. **Don't use household cleaners.** Dish soap, glass cleaner, or anything with harsh chemicals will strip your wax or coating and won't effectively neutralise the acid.

If you're caught without any products, spit actually works in a pinch — it's slightly alkaline and enzymatic. Not glamorous, but any detailer will tell you the same thing.

## What If the Damage Is Already Done?

If you find a ring-shaped mark on your paint after removing a dropping, you're looking at a Stage 1 etch. Light etching can often be removed with a single-stage polish — something a trained hand with a machine polisher can address without cutting through the clear coat.

Deeper damage — where the surface feels physically textured even after polishing — requires a two-stage paint correction. At that point, you're looking at professional intervention.

The critical thing to know: etching almost never repairs itself. Unlike light swirls that can look better under certain lighting, acid etching is a physical change to the clear coat surface. It doesn't improve over time.

## Does Ceramic Coating Help?

Yes, meaningfully. A properly applied ceramic coating creates a sacrificial layer above your clear coat that absorbs the initial acid contact. It doesn't make your car immune to bird droppings — you still need to remove them promptly — but it dramatically extends the window you have to act before etching reaches the clear coat.

Think of it this way: instead of 10 minutes on bare paint, a coated car in the same Melbourne sun conditions gives you closer to 30–45 minutes before damage reaches the clear coat. That's the difference between a stressful moment and a genuine problem.

![Ceramic coating being professionally applied for long-term paint protection](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1619767886558-efdc259cde1a?w=1200&q=80)

PPF (paint protection film) goes a step further — it's a physical barrier that absorbs both the chemical and mechanical impact of droppings. For park-all-day situations in leafy suburbs like Hawthorn or Kew, PPF on the bonnet and roof is a practical choice worth considering.

## The Bottom Line

Bird droppings are one of the most common causes of localised paint damage we see — and almost entirely avoidable with a fast response. Keep a spray bottle and a microfibre cloth in your car. Act within 10 minutes when you can. If you park under trees regularly in Melbourne's leafier suburbs, look at your protection options before the damage makes the decision for you.

The repair always costs more than the prevention.
