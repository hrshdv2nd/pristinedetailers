July in Melbourne is harder on unprotected paint than most owners realise — cold overnight temperatures, condensation sitting on the bonnet, and bird droppings that etch faster in wet conditions.

If you've been wondering whether to get ceramic coating or PPF, here's the straight answer: they're not competing products, they do different jobs. Ceramic bonds chemically to your clear coat and repels water, bird droppings, UV, and most contaminants for years — up to 8, manufacturer-warranted. One handles what falls on your car, the other handles what hits it at speed.

For a car worth protecting, the right strategy uses both — PPF on the high-impact areas first, ceramic over the whole car on top.

## Platform: LinkedIn

**Caption:**

A question worth asking before your next service interval: is your car protected against what's actually going to damage it?

Most car owners don't distinguish between the two distinct threats to paintwork. Physical impact — stone chips from Melbourne freeways, car park scuffs, gravel on the Mornington Peninsula run — damages paint mechanically. Chemical and environmental degradation — bird droppings, UV exposure, acid rain, oxidation through Melbourne's changing seasons — damages it over time. These require different solutions, and most people are only addressing one of them.

PPF (paint protection film) handles physical damage: a self-healing polyurethane film applied over vulnerable panels that absorbs chips and impacts before they reach the paint. It's optically clear and carries a manufacturer warranty. Ceramic coating handles environmental damage: a nano-ceramic layer bonded chemically to the clear coat, hydrophobic and UV-stable, warranted up to 8 years. The correct protection strategy uses both, in the right order — PPF on the high-impact zones first, ceramic over the complete car.

For anyone running a vehicle worth $60K or more, this is asset management, not cosmetics.
