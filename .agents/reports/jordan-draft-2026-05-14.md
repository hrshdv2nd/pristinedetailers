You park under a tree in South Yarra, walk into a meeting, come back an hour later to find a white-grey smear across your bonnet. Most people grab a tissue and wipe. That's the worst thing you can do.

Bird droppings are one of the most underestimated threats to automotive paint in Australia — and Melbourne's parks, bay foreshore suburbs, and council-planted trees make this a near-daily risk for anyone parking outdoors. Here's what's actually happening when a bird scores a direct hit on your clear coat, and why the clock starts ticking the moment it lands.

## What's Actually in Bird Droppings

The white paste isn't just waste. Bird droppings are a concentrated mix of uric acid (pH 3.5–4.5), undigested seeds and berries, bile, and digestive enzymes. It's the uric acid that does the damage — it's corrosive enough to etch into automotive clear coat within minutes in warm conditions.

Melbourne's summer heat accelerates this dramatically. On a 35-degree day in Toorak, your car's bonnet surface can reach 60–70°C. At those temperatures, uric acid doesn't just sit on the surface — it softens the clear coat, expands into micro-pores, and as the dropping dries and shrinks, it pulls the clear coat with it. The result is a pitted, dull impression that mirrors the exact shape of the dropping. Detailers call it etching.

## The 10-Minute Window

In direct sunlight at 30°C+, etching can begin in under 10 minutes. In shade or cooler weather, you have longer — sometimes 30 minutes to a couple of hours. But you rarely know when the dropping landed, which means by the time you've noticed it, you may already be in damage territory.

The popular advice to "soak it first" is correct — but what you use and how you wipe matters enormously. A dry tissue or paper towel introduces micro-scratches into your clear coat as you drag abrasive particles across the surface. Water alone softens the dropping but doesn't neutralise the acid.

The correct approach:

1. **Flood it with water** — use a bottle, not a mist spray. You want volume to dilute the acid.
2. **Let it sit for 30–60 seconds** to fully soften and loosen the material.
3. **Blot with a clean microfibre cloth** — never wipe, never rub. Lift straight off.
4. **Apply a quick detailer spray** if you have one, and blot again.
5. **Inspect the area** — if there's a visible dull mark, you may already have a light etch.

The goal is to dilute the acid and lift the material off the paint with zero lateral friction. Any dragging motion risks both scratching the clear coat and smearing acid across a wider area.

## Why Some Cars Are More Vulnerable

Not all paint responds the same. Factory clear coats vary significantly in hardness and thickness. Soft clear coats — common on many European vehicles, particularly older BMWs and certain Volkswagen Group cars — etch faster and deeper than harder Japanese clear coats. Tesla's factory paint is notoriously soft, which is one reason new Tesla owners should be thinking about paint protection from day one.

Single-stage paint (found on some Porsches and older classics) is even more exposed — there's no clear coat to sacrifice. The acid hits the colour layer directly.

## What Ceramic Coating Actually Changes

A properly applied ceramic coating doesn't make your car immune to bird droppings — nothing does. What it does is raise the surface barrier and buy you time. The coating is chemically harder than most factory clear coats, so the acid has to work through the coating before reaching your paint. It also makes the surface hydrophobic and non-porous, so the dropping sits on top rather than immediately bonding to the surface.

This is a genuine real-world advantage. The difference between a coated and uncoated car isn't theoretical — it's the difference between blotting off a dropping with no visible damage versus scheduling a spot paint correction.

That said, ceramic coatings aren't acid-proof indefinitely. A dropping left on a coated car in full Melbourne sun for a full afternoon can still etch the coating itself — which then requires machine polishing to restore clarity. The coating takes the hit instead of your paint, but it still needs attention.

## The Practical Fix

Keep a small kit in your car: a 500ml bottle of clean water, two microfibre cloths, and a spray bottle of diluted quick detailer. This costs under $30 and takes up less room than a drink bottle. In Melbourne summers, it will pay for itself many times over.

If you're already seeing dull, hazy marks where droppings have been, that's an etch — and it needs paint correction. Machine polishing removes the damaged layer of clear coat and restores the surface. Leave it too long and the etch deepens past what polishing can fix. At that point, you're looking at a respray.

The bird isn't the problem. Waiting is.
