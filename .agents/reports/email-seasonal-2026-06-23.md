Hi [First name],

School holidays start this week, and if you're planning a drive to the Mornington Peninsula, the Surf Coast, or anywhere south of Frankston — this is worth two minutes of your time.

The Nepean Highway in July is one of the worst roads in Victoria for paint damage. Not because of potholes (though there are those too). Because of the combination of things happening at the same time:

**Stone chips.** Traffic is heavier during holidays. More trucks, more SUVs, more gravel kicked up from the road shoulders. Freeway driving at 100km/h means every chip that reaches your paint does so with serious force. A PPF-wrapped bonnet doesn't notice. Unprotected paint records every single one.

**Salt spray.** Once you're past Frankston and near the bay, the coastal air carries salt. It settles on lower panels, door sills, and underneath the car. It doesn't wash off with rain — rain actually helps it bond. If you're doing the Mornington Peninsula circuit or heading down to Sorrento or Portsea, your undercarriage is getting a salt treatment whether you asked for one or not.

**Cold overnight parking.** If you're staying a night or two, the car sits outside in coastal winter air. Cold, damp, salt-laden. The condensation cycle that builds up over a weekend on the coast is the equivalent of a week of suburban driving in terms of coating stress.

## Copy Notes

**Why this works:**

- **The hook is geographically specific.** "Nepean Highway in July" is a sentence every Melbourne driver reads and nods at. It's not generic winter advice — it's a road they've driven, with trucks they remember sitting behind. Specificity earns trust.

- **Three-threat structure is concrete.** Stone chips, salt spray, overnight condensation — each has a mechanism. Readers understand *why* it's a problem, not just that it is. Educated customers book more and cancel less.

- **The framing is anticipatory, not retrospective.** Most retention emails talk about damage that's already happened. This one says: something is about to happen. That creates a different kind of urgency — one that feels like a useful heads-up from a friend, not a scare tactic.

- **The "road trip" frame reaches people who've gone quiet.** Customers who haven't thought about their car all winter will think about it now because they're actively planning the drive. The holiday is the trigger. We're just showing up at the right moment.

- **Three segments, one email.** The middle section addresses members, coated customers, and uncoated customers in three short paragraphs. Each person reads the one paragraph that applies to them and the rest still reinforces the brand. No need for three separate emails.

- **Tone: friendly expert.** "Drive safe down there" does a lot of work — it's warm without being soft. It's the thing a knowledgeable mate would say before you left, not a brand trying to seem relatable.

- **Reply CTA is the unsung hero.** The reply ask ("reply with what you're driving") converts the undecided reader into a warm conversation. These convert better than cold clicks because by the time they reply, they're already engaged.

**What this email is NOT doing:**
- Not trying to close everyone on a full ceramic package
- Not repeating the mould/condensation angle from the last two sends
- Not using urgency that feels manufactured — the school holidays date is real
