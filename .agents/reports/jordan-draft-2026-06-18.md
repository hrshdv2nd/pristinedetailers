If you drive on Melbourne's Eastern Freeway, Eastlink, or the Monash, stone chips are not a matter of if — they're a matter of when. Paint Protection Film (PPF) is the only product that physically absorbs impact before it reaches your paint. No coating, wax, or sealant does this.

## Why Melbourne Freeways Are Particularly Brutal

Melbourne's freeway network combines three factors that make paint damage nearly inevitable for daily drivers: loose aggregate, heavy truck traffic, and high-speed lanes with minimal distance between vehicles.

The Eastern Freeway through Balwyn and Bulleen runs through ongoing construction zones — the North East Link project has added significant debris scatter to an already high-risk corridor. Eastlink south through Dandenong has loose gravel sections at on-ramps where aggregate migrates from verges onto the travel lane. The Monash through Clayton and Oakleigh sees some of the highest truck density in Victoria.

Gravel and loose stone get thrown from tyres of vehicles travelling at 100–110 km/h. At that speed, a 5 mm piece of aggregate hits your bonnet with enough force to breach the clear coat in a single impact. Once that outer layer is broken, moisture and UV reach the base coat directly.

## What Happens to Unprotected Paint

A stone chip that breaks through the clear coat exposes the base coat — and often the primer beneath it. In Melbourne's coastal climate, with salt air from Port Phillip Bay reaching as far inland as Richmond and Hawthorn, bare metal oxidises fast. A chip left untreated for two to three months in bayside suburbs can show rust bloom at the edges.

Paint correction can restore cosmetic damage, but it cannot replace lost clear coat thickness. A car that has been chipped repeatedly has thinner protection at those sites — and less material for a polisher to work with if correction is needed later.

## What PPF Actually Does

Paint Protection Film is a polyurethane film — typically 6–8 mil thick — applied directly to your paint using a precision-cut pattern for your vehicle's make and model. The film sits between your paint and the road environment.

When a stone hits PPF, the energy disperses through the film rather than concentrating on the clear coat. Quality films from manufacturers like Xpel, SunTek, and LLumar include a self-healing top coat. Minor surface abrasions — light swirls from car washes, contact marks — disappear with heat exposure, either from sunlight or a rinse with warm water.

Partial coverage makes sense for most daily drivers: the bonnet, front guards, and mirrors take approximately 80% of freeway debris. Full-body wrap is worth considering for prestige vehicles, track cars, or any vehicle where long-term resale value needs protecting.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does PPF stop all stone chips?**
PPF stops the vast majority of impacts a freeway creates. Extremely high-energy impacts — a direct rock thrown by a truck at close range — can still penetrate. But the chip ends up in the film, not in your paint. The film can be replaced; repainted panels permanently lose resale value.

**Can PPF be added after a chip already appears?**
Yes. The panel is prepped first — chips are filled and colour-matched before film is applied. The existing chip won't spread, and the surface is protected from further damage. The original chip may be faintly visible under the film, but it won't worsen.

**How long does PPF last on high-impact zones?**
Quality film on a properly prepped surface lasts 8–10 years with basic maintenance. The film wears before your paint does — which is the point. When the film is due for replacement, the paint underneath is still factory condition.
